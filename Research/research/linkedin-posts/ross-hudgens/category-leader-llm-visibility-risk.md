# Being a category leader can sometimes negatively impact your LLM visibility.

Being a category leader can sometimes negatively impact your LLM visibility.

Historically, dominant brands in-market have had very little reason to pay well in their affiliate programs. If everyone is going to end up buying your product, why should you provide a significant cut to the middleman in that transaction?

An example of this is Amazon. They started with best-in-class rates. Over the years, they slowly dwindled to near-nothing. But most still used them, because there was no other option.

In the LLM influence world, affiliates will often include category leaders even if they pay poor commissions because they convert so well, and it'd be weird if they didn't. But not always. 

Many will make the explicit decision to instead go with a counter-factual angle. "Excel is dead". "QuickBooks isn't worth using." "The iPhone isn't the best smartphone." 

(maybe this will create a fight in the comments) 

I don't know about you, but Excel is still a great product for many, especially those in finance. QuickBooks is an amazing fit for Siege. I also love my iPhone (besides the addiction part).

Not always are the takes this inflammatory, but even in those situations, the subtle drags have the potential to compound. If one pays 3x the other, even if the creator decides not to disparage them, they're more likely to call another the best overall. Or rank them a little lower.

Of course, these are just variables in a larger equation. Being category-dominant means these signals may not be enough to change the tide. But sometimes they are. And sometimes they will on the unseen long-tail, too.

The action for brands? Self-awareness of this dynamic. How are active affiliates in your market treating you? How big of an influence are they having on the prompts that matter? 

If the impact is outsized, you may want to consider shifting your affiliate program standards, even selectively, to adjust sentiment in your favor. 

Otherwise you may be at risk of finding yourself at the whim of an excessively clickable thumbnail at some point in the near future.
