# I once had a university English professor who would constantly say, "you'll always find what you're looking for."

I once had a university English professor who would constantly say, "you'll always find what you're looking for." 

This was in reference to analyzing a text. In other words, whatever lens you use to analyze a book, you'll find evidence that backs up your view. 

Large datasets introduce a similar problem, namely through spurious correlations, but also through cherry picked views, heterogenous comparisons, and a general lack of consensus on methodology. 

That's what I see happening with AI search, where there's such an abundance of data, but that data seems to be making things only more confusing. 

Essentially, whatever you do or sell, you can find a view of the data that suggests what you do or sell is important for AI search visibility. Reddit is a top citation source, so you need community management. Owned content pales in comparison to off page citations, so media outreach, PR, press releases, review sites and customer marketing, or [insert tactic here] is what really matters. LinkedIn is now a prominent citation source, so.... 

End of the day, you've got to sit down and look at your own data, think critically, and construct a strategy that wins specifically, not broadly.

Nassim Taleb put it best:

"We're more fooled by noise than ever before, and it's because of a nasty phenomenon called "big data." With big data, researchers have brought cherry-picking to an industrial level.

Modernity provides too many variables, but too little data per variable. So the spurious relationships grow much, much faster than real information.

In other words: Big data may mean more information, but it also means more false information."

## Annotation

**Claim:** The abundance of AI search citation data makes strategy more confusing, not clearer — because any tactic can be justified by cherry-picking the right dataset, the only reliable foundation is your own vertical-specific data analyzed critically.
